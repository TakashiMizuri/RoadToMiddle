## Answers

### 1. [Recall]
**Answer:** Создаёт/уничтожает handler pool каждый раз → нет connection reuse → накопление TIME_WAIT и exhaustion ephemeral ports под load.

**Why:** Core socket exhaustion mechanism.

---

### 2. [Recall]
**Answer:** Состояние TCP после close; локальный port недоступен для reuse некоторое время → при массовых коротких connections порты заканчиваются.

**Why:** Explains delayed recovery after spike.

---

### 3. [Understanding]
**Answer:** Disposable ≠ short-lived; expensive resource — **handler pool**; intended for long reuse; dispose on shutdown not per operation.

**Why:** Official HttpClient guidelines narrative.

---

### 4. [Understanding]
**Answer:** Часть запросов к старому IP; intermittent failures until process restart or connection lifetime forces re-resolve.

**Why:** DNS tied to handler lifetime.

---

### 5. [Understanding]
**Answer:** Periodic new connections trigger **DNS re-resolution** and avoid infinitely old connections; security/TLS rotation side benefits.

**Why:** Factory handler rotation rationale.

---

### 6. [Application]
**Answer:** **`PooledConnectionLifetime`** (e.g. 5 min); **`MaxConnectionsPerServer`** (≥15 or tuned); optional idle timeout.

**Why:** DNS + fan-out control.

---

### 7. [Application]
**Answer:** Inject **`IHttpClientFactory`**; **`var client = factory.CreateClient("name")`** once per iteration or capture outside loop — shares handler pool.

**Why:** Standard ASP.NET fix.

---

### 8. [Analysis]
**Answer:** **Stale DNS** after deploy; no handler rotation; harder to configure per-service handlers; factory adds named clients and Polly integration.

**Why:** Singleton vs factory trade-off.

---

**Answer:** **No** — short-lived client object from factory is fine; don't **`using dispose`** each time as if new handler each call; factory manages handler lifetime.

**Why:** Common misuse after learning IDisposable rule.

**Common mistake:** Disposing CreateClient() every call thinking it helps.
